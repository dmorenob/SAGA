<b> Article Number: </b>0944<br><b> Period: </b>3<br>URL: http://marketbiznews.blogspot.com/2009/11/sharp-housing-construction-decline-send.html<br>DATE: 2009-11-18<br>Author: unknown.<br><br>Title.: sharp housing construction decline send investors into tailspin<br><br> Housing Construction and Technology Report Depresses the Market<br>AP News - Investors turned cautious Wednesday as an unexpected drop in home construction and disappointing forecasts from technology companies raised concerns about the pace of the economy's recovery.<br><br>The modest drop came a day after major stock indicators closed at 13-month highs, including the Dow Jones industrial average, which has risen 9 of the past 10 days. Analysts said the quick ascent means the market is due for a rest.<br><br>John Brady, senior vice president of global interest rate products at MF Global in Chicago, said as the end of the year approaches traders are looking foremost at preserving the gains amassed in an eight-month rally that has given the benchmark Standard & Poor's 500 index a gain of 22.9 percent for the year.<br>"It's a bit of a consolidation trade," he said. "Traders are scared to go out<br>too far out on a limb here and do anything too risky late in the year."<br><br>The day's economic news added to investors' caution. The Commerce Department said construction of homes and apartments fell 10.6 percent in October to an annual rate of 529,000, well below the pace of 600,000 that economists polled by Thomson Reuters expected.<br>Joe Heider, president of Dawson Wealth Management in Cleveland, said the disappointing results "will push against what was a very bullish attitude on Wall Street."<br><br>Heider said investors were trying to determine whether the slowdown signaled weakness in the economy or a reluctance among builders to break ground when the future of a homebuyers' tax credit was uncertain. Lawmakers extended a tax credit for first-time homebuyers that was set to end this month through June.<br><br>Building permits, a key indication for future activity, slid 4 percent to an annual rate of 552,000, also below the rate of 580,000 that analysts had forecast.<br><br>Technology shares fell after BMO Capital Markets said Blackberry maker Research in Motion Ltd. faces increased competition as consumers opt for less expensive phones. Meanwhile, forecasts from software makers Autodesk Inc. and Salesforce.com fell short of analysts expectations.<br><br>In early afternoon trading, the Dow fell 59.93, or 0.6 percent, to 10,377.49. The broader S&P 500 index fell 5.45, or 0.5 percent, to 1,104.87, while the technology-heavy Nasdaq composite index fell 19.92, or 0.9 percent, to 2,183.86.<br><br>There was little reaction a report that found inflation at the retail level remained tame as rising unemployment, nervous consumers and tight credit keep prices stable.<br><br>The Labor Department said consumer prices rose 0.3 percent in October, slightly above the 0.2 percent economists expected. Core inflation, which excludes volatile energy and food prices, rose 0.2 percent, compared to expectations of a 0.1 percent rise.<br><br>Investors are looking for any signals of further improvement in the economy to justify the gains that pulled major stock indexes off 12-year lows in March. Rising unemployment and tepid retail sales have some analysts worried that investors might have been too quick to place bets on a recovery.<br><br>
